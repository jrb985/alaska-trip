The finding that reshaped this trip

You cannot ride to Nome.

Nome has no road connection to the North American highway system. It is a fly-in / barge-in town. No highway reaches it from Fairbanks, Anchorage, or anywhere else.

Nome does have roughly 250–350 miles of its own gravel roads — Teller, Council, Kougarok — but that network is an island. It dead-ends into tundra and connects to nothing.

The original goal was Nome. To ride in Nome you would have to air-freight the bike from Anchorage or barge it in. So the destination changed.

Decision

Deadhorse / Prudhoe Bay via the Dalton Highway. It is road-continuous from Seattle, it is genuinely the end of the road, and it is the trip most people mean by this. Nome is parked as a possible future add-on.

Route

Seattle → BC → Yukon → Fairbanks → Deadhorse

Ride a loop: north one corridor, south the other. Same mileage, double the country.

Northbound — Stewart–Cassiar (BC-37)

~450 mi. Remote, quiet, fully paved. More wildlife, fewer services. The side trip to Stewart/Hyder is worth it.

Southbound — Alaska Highway (Alcan)

1,387 mi, Dawson Creek → Delta Junction. More services, more traffic. The historic route.

The Dalton Highway

Everything south of Fairbanks is a normal trip. This isn't.

414 miles, Livengood → Deadhorse. Majority unpaved. No cell service.

Trucks own the road

Heavy oilfield traffic. Get over, slow down, expect flying rock. Truck-thrown stone is the number one source of damage.

Calcium chloride

The surface is treated with it for dust control. Dry, it packs hard, feels like pavement, and lulls you. Wet, it turns to greasy peanut butter and it will put you down. It is also brutally corrosive — wash the bike, underside especially, as soon as you're off it.

Atigun Pass · 4,739 ft

Weather can be full winter in any month. No services, no signal, no help.

Pacing

Don't do it in one heroic push. Four days from Fairbanks and back: Fairbanks → Coldfoot, Coldfoot → Deadhorse, Deadhorse → Coldfoot, Coldfoot → Fairbanks. Add a day to take the Arctic Ocean shuttle without rushing.

Constraint #1

The fuel gap is longer than the bike's range.

Fuel exists at exactly three places on the Dalton.

Coldfoot → Deadhorse

The longest fuel gap on any public road in the United States.

239 mi  fuel gap
230 mi  stock range

The F650GS holds 4.4 US gallons and returns roughly 50 mpg — about 230 miles claimed. Derate for a loaded bike, headwind, gravel, and low-gear slogging through wet calcium chloride, and the real number is closer to 200.

You would run dry before Deadhorse.

And you can't fix it the usual way. The F650GS fuel tank sits under the seat, integrated into the frame's rear triangle. There is no IMS/Safari-style long-range tank for this bike — the aftermarket effectively doesn't exist, and the under-seat aux tanks people have attempted fight a compound-curved seat base and custom fuel plumbing.

Constraint #1 — solved

Carry external fuel. It's a $100 problem.

ConfigurationTotal rangeVerdict
Stock tank only~200–230 miNot enough
+ 1 gal Rotopax~280 miSolves it

Recommendation

One to two gallons of Rotopax on a rear rack, carried on the Dalton only. Cheap, simple, proven. Leave it behind for the pavement miles south of Fairbanks.

Discipline

Top off at Yukon River (MP 56) and Coldfoot (MP 175) every time, without exception. There is no "I'll get it at the next one."

Verdict: a good choice

A proven round-the-world bike. Comfortable over the 2,400 pavement miles north, and light enough to pick up alone — which you will need to do. This is not a compromise pick.

Base, not Dakar

19" front and shorter travel is a modest disadvantage on the Dalton versus the Dakar's 21". Not a problem — just ride slower and stand on the pegs.

Mechanical refresh — done

Completed before July 2026. This retires the single biggest risk in the plan.

The bike is 24 years old

Reliability is a safety item, not a maintenance item. A breakdown on the Dalton is an emergency: no cell service, 240 miles between services, and a tow from Deadhorse costs more than the bike is worth.

Known F650GS failure points

  • Fuel pump + controller — a known failure on this generation, in-tank under the seat. Fails on the Dalton and the trip is over in the worst possible place. Carry a spare.
  • Fuel-level "strip" sensor — famously fails. Normally a shrug, ride by trip meter. But the range is already short of the 239-mile gap, so flying blind on fuel is not acceptable here. Fix it.
  • Water pump seal — classic wear item. Check the weep hole for coolant.
  • Stator / regulator-rectifier — check charging voltage.
  • Valve clearances checked and set.

Tires

Don't optimize for the 8% of the trip that's dirt.

Round trip is about 6,000 miles. The Dalton is roughly 600 of them. A soft knobby rear can be cooked in ~3,000 miles — you'd destroy it just getting to Fairbanks.

The real constraint is the 140/80-17 rear, which is an unusual size with very few options — not grip.

Heidenau K60 Scout

Comes in 140/80-17. Riders report 10,000+ miles on a set. Adequate on the Dalton, excellent everywhere else. One set does the whole trip. Downside: notoriously hard to mount.

Recommended

Continental TKC80

Better in mud, but wears fast — and Continental is phasing it out, replaced by the redesigned TKC80² (small sizes May 2026, larger August 2026).

Gen-2 only

Mitas E-07

More like 60/40 street, long-wearing. Fine — but the K60 does the same job with more dirt capability.

Redundant

Recommendation

Heidenau K60 Scout front and rear, one fresh set, the whole trip. No mid-trip swap, no shipping tires to Fairbanks. Ride the Dalton a little slower to make up the grip difference — which you should be doing anyway.

Verify early

The 140/80-17 rear is an oddball size with few options. Confirm availability before building around it. If that size dries up, this section changes.

The destination

Deadhorse is an industrial camp, not a town.

No beach, no bar, no triumphant sign worth much. Manage expectations — the ride is the reward.

Lodging — book ahead, they sell out

Aurora Hotel · Prudhoe Bay Hotel · Deadhorse Camp. Rustic, camp-style, meals usually included. Camping there is effectively not a thing — budget for a room.

You cannot ride to the Arctic Ocean

The final stretch crosses secured oilfield property. You must take an escorted shuttle, booked in advance — 24h minimum for the security check. Bring government photo ID.

Gear, safety & border

The non-negotiables

Safety

  • Satellite communicator (inReach / ZOLEO). No cell on the Dalton — this is the only way to call for help.
  • Bear spray, accessible, not buried in a pannier.
  • Ability to fix a flat yourself: tubes, irons, pump.
  • First aid kit, plus a day of emergency food and water.

Gear

  • Layers rated for near-freezing and rain.
  • Heated grips / vest — worth it.
  • Real rain gear. You will ride in rain.
  • Head net and DEET.
  • Camping kit rated well below expected lows.

Border — you cross into Canada twice

  • Passport — not just a passport card.
  • Proof of insurance valid in Canada. US policies are not automatically valid; confirm with your insurer.
  • Vehicle registration. Cash in CAD and USD — remote stops can be card-flaky.

Firearms: do not. Bringing a handgun into Canada is a serious offense and will end the trip at the border. Bear spray is the answer.
